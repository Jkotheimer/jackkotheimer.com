Roots

I've been playing guitar since I was 11 and drums since I was 15. Music has always been a pretty big part of my life and I've been in several bands in my life. I've shifted genres over time from metal and punk over towards the alternative and indie side. The first band I was ever in played "That's What You Get" by Paramore at our high school talent show and we surprisingly won! It was my first time playing drums live. I wish I had a video of it... But I've played in several bands since then, and I just love playing drums on stage.

Recently

Ever since college started for me, I haven't been able to play as much as I'd like to. I had to quit my bands and focus on school. That's okay with me though because every time I visit my parents, I go to the basement and jam out on the drums for a bit to satiate my constant urge to whack shit with wooden sticks in a sporatic, yet structured and controlled manner. I did, however, get the chance to write and record a song for one of my english classes. We were instructed to come up with a creative project related to language, so I wrote a song about how speaking up has always been tough for me and how I learned to overcome that. Warning: the audio quality is ~terrible~ because I didn't have access to a real drum kit or guitar amp and I also wrote and recorded the whole thing in a 12 hour one-nighter the day before it was due.

Rubik's Cubes

When I was a child, my sister was a special olympics swimmer. My dad and I would always go to her swim meets to watch her swim. As you may or may not know, swim meets take ~forever~, so my dad and I would always bring activities and snacks for us to do while waiting for her races to begin. We always brought a Rubik's cube with us and try to solve it. One day, we eventually finished it. We solved the Rubiks's cube in a hot, chlorine filled pool room at Stagg High School. Ever since that day, I've been absolutely obsessed with the idea of solving a Rubik's cube as fast as I can. So far, my fastest time is 12.88 seconds. On average, I'm usually around 20-25 seconds, but that one time, I got lucky with an OLL and PLL skip. Since I've sorta plateaued with a 3x3 cube, I expanded to 4x4s, 5x5s, etc. I once got my hands on a 7x7 cube and it took me a little over an hour to solve. In high school, I played with them so much that it pissed off my parents and teachers. I kept skipping my homework assignments and refusing to study because I was so obsessed. Nowadays, I rarely find time to play with them, but if I ever need a mental break from school, coding, or work, I'll pick up a cube and solve it as many times as I can in a 15 minute block.

Arch Linux

If you've ever tried to install Arch Linux, you know that it is crazy hard. Like it took me forever to figure it out. I love the fact that it took forever. It's a puzzle. In my Sophomore year of college, I decided to delve into the world of Linux. After a week or so of research, I could install Ubuntu and Mint on my own. It's a shallow learning curve, but it was a good way for me to dip my toes in. I got a little too ambitious after that first week and decided to jump right into Arch Linux. 'Twas a poor decision. I very quickly learned how little I know. No GUI?!?! What am I a neanderthal? I stuck with Ubuntu for a bit. I took a Summer to learn Bash and what each directory in the root filesystem of Linux means. Come my Jeanior year (junior/senior since I graduated a year early), I decided to revisit Arch Linux. I tried ~so~ many times. Like hundreds. "What the fuck is an fstab?" "What the hell is a chroot?" "How the FUCK do I connect to the internet?" This was a tough puzzle to crack. Yes, I know, I know, the Arch Wiki has literally everything you need to know. But when you're a noob like I was, the Arch Wiki is written in heiroglyphics. Eventually, I finally got it installed. At least to the point that I could boot into the tty1 terminal. No desktop manager yet. Now, at this time, I knew of the race war that exists between GNOME and KDE users. Since I'd been using GNOME on Ubuntu for a few months, I figured it wouldn't hurt to try KDE Plasma. My apologies to the GNOME fanboys, but Plasma is fuckin awesome. Anywhomst, the installation process of Arch Linux was such a fun process of puzzles. I still run Arch on all my machines to this day (except for the Raspberry Pi upon which this webserver is deployed).
